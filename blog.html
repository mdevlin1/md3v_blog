A Story of Side Projects

Post by Mitchell Devlin, Nov 05, 2022.
Story Time Programming

My coworkers think that I am crazy for wanting to explore programming projects outside of work. They say that doing something that so closely resembles your professional career outside of work will ultimately lead to more burnout (as if we didn't have enough already). I understand where they are coming from, but I got into this line of work because I loved to figure out how technology worked, and tinkering with my own projects. Over the years, I have to admit that the wonder and amazement that I felt when I started learning about programming (over 10 years ago, wow I'm old now lol) has abated slightly. When you're stuck in the third redundant meeting in a row explaining the same concepts to your superiors over and over again, then it starts to feel less magical. However, that is in fact the exact reason that I advocate for developers to pick up side projects and explore outside of their realm of expertise more. It's opening up pandoras box once again. You get to rediscover the feeling that brought us all into this field in the first place.

Recently, I picked up a long forgotten and in dire need of attention project. When I was a wee developer, I loved writing little tools and games in Python. It was a fantastic language for a relatively inexperienced developer to pick up and run with. There is plenty of great documentation and the syntax is forgiving enough and intuitive for a newbie developer to create some cool software. I started to implement the classic arcade game Snake in pygame. For the uninitiated, pygame is a set of python modules for writing games in, and adds functionality to SDL (a popular graphics library). To my credit, the rendition of Snake that I created did work, and looked relatively polished for someone who didn't really know what they were doing. Under the hood was a different story. I have learned a lot about maintainability and function decomposition since I created this game. It was a complete mess down there.

I had my work cut out for me. Everything was in one giant main function. The entire event based system in one unorganized mess of a function. The variable names didn't make sense. There were magic numbers everywhere. The screen resolution was locked to 800x600 and I did some unintelligible math to determine if the user had won the game and filled up the hardcoded size space with their snake. I wanted to trash the damn thing and start over. Eventually I managed to scrounge up enough motivation to untangle the spaghetti and make it programmer friendly. Now, was this all worth it? Probably not, I don't really care about some stupid project that I hobbled together a decade ago. The real reason I took up the endeavour was because it was on my public github page which was linked on my resume. Somehow my current employer saw this and decided there was some method to the madness, or more probable just didn't bother to look.... Now that the project is in a better state, I'm thinking of expanding it and developing some AI that can win the game in the most efficient way possible. It could take advantage of some fancy path finding algorithms and be a cool addition to an otherwise blah project.
